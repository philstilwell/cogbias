Cognitive Bias

Outcome bias

The tendency to judge the quality of a decision mainly by how things turned out rather than by the quality of the reasoning under the uncertainty that existed at the time.

Memory & Retrospection Structured process Postmortems & learningTeams & management

Default mental move

Score the decision by the ending instead of by the information and reasoning available before the ending.

What it distorts

It confuses luck with judgment and makes postmortems misleading.

Common trigger

Performance reviews, investing, leadership evaluation, and public retrospective blame.

First countermove

Separate process review from outcome review and ask whether the same decision would be endorsed if the result had flipped.

Mechanism

Outcomes are concrete and emotionally loud, while process quality is abstract and harder to see. Success launders shaky reasoning; failure stains good reasoning.

Signals You Are In It

  • Good outcomes excuse bad process or bad outcomes eclipse solid process.
  • The review begins with the ending before reconstructing the decision context.
  • Luck is talked about only when it benefits the side we dislike.

Why This Category Matters

These biases rewrite the past after outcomes arrive, making learning look cleaner than it was.

Are we remembering the decision process fairly, or only remembering the ending?

Counter It

From detection to intervention

Bias work is less about slapping on a label and more about changing what happens next in the decision process.

Spot It

  • Ask whether the same reasoning would be praised if the outcome were worse.
  • Check whether process quality has been independently inspected.
  • Look for decisions that seem wise only because the coin landed heads.

Slow It

  • Run separate process and outcome scorecards.
  • Use premortems and decision journals to document the original reasoning.
  • Compare this case to others with similar process but different luck.

Reframe It

  • Treat outcomes as noisy feedback, not as pure verdicts on judgment.
  • Ask whether the process was robust under the uncertainty that actually existed.
  • Remember that good bets can lose and bad bets can win.
