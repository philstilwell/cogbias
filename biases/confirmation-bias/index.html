Cognitive Bias

Confirmation bias

The tendency to notice, seek, and remember evidence that supports the story you already prefer more readily than evidence that threatens it.

Belief Protection Structured process Media & politicsResearch & evidence

Default mental move

Protect the current belief by sampling supportive cases first.

What it distorts

It makes the evidence stream look more one-sided than it really is.

Common trigger

Identity-charged topics and early hunches formed under uncertainty.

First countermove

Write down what evidence would seriously lower confidence before searching for more support.

Mechanism

Once a hypothesis feels plausible, attention and memory become selective. Friendly evidence feels weighty while counterevidence feels weak, atypical, or unimportant.

Signals You Are In It

  • You can recall supporting examples much faster than disconfirming ones.
  • Counterexamples are dismissed before they are investigated.
  • Searching stops as soon as the preferred story fits.

Why This Category Matters

These biases stabilize the stories we already prefer, especially when identity or commitment is involved.

What would this person have to see before they would seriously revise the current view?

Counter It

From detection to intervention

Bias work is less about slapping on a label and more about changing what happens next in the decision process.

Spot It

  • Ask which live alternative explanations were actively tested rather than merely mentioned.
  • Check whether the evidence list is mostly made of confirming cases.
  • Notice whether disconfirming data is being screened out on quality grounds only after it points the wrong way.

Slow It

  • Generate two rival hypotheses before collecting the next round of evidence.
  • Assign one person to build the strongest countercase.
  • Use the same evidence standards for friendly and unfriendly cases.

Reframe It

  • Treat belief revision as progress rather than loss.
  • Ask which result would most surprise you and go look there first.
  • Reword the question from 'How do I defend this?' to 'What best explains the full pattern?'

Motivated reasoning
