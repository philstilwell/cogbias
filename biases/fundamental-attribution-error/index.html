Cognitive Bias

Fundamental attribution error

The tendency to explain other people's behavior too quickly in terms of character while underweighting situational pressures and constraints.

Social Judgment Quick reset Teams & managementMedia & politics

Default mental move

Read the act as a window into the person more than into the situation.

What it distorts

It turns local behavior into overly global character judgments.

Common trigger

Conflict, disappointment, public mistakes, and thin background knowledge.

First countermove

List three situational explanations before settling on a trait explanation.

Mechanism

Behavior is vivid; context is often hidden. The mind reaches for trait stories because they feel coherent and socially portable.

Signals You Are In It

  • A single bad action quickly becomes a statement about what the person is like.
  • Context is treated as excuse-making only after the blame story is in place.
  • Your own behavior is explained situationally while others' behavior is explained dispositionally.

Why This Category Matters

These biases simplify people too quickly by overreading character, traits, or first impressions.

What situational explanation or hidden context might this snap judgment be ignoring?

Counter It

From detection to intervention

Bias work is less about slapping on a label and more about changing what happens next in the decision process.

Spot It

  • Ask what constraints, incentives, or missing information the person may have been under.
  • Check whether the explanation would change if the actor were yourself or an ally.
  • Notice whether the leap from act to trait is faster than the evidence allows.

Slow It

  • Separate observed behavior, inferred motives, and character judgments into different columns.
  • Investigate situational pressures before discussing personality.
  • Look for repeated cross-situational patterns before making stable trait claims.

Reframe It

  • Replace 'That shows who they are' with 'That is one behavior under one set of conditions.'
  • Treat context as causal information, not as automatic exoneration.
  • Remember that opaque situations create harsh person-level stories.

Related biases

These nearby patterns often travel with fundamental attribution error or get confused with it.

Halo effect

The tendency for one salient positive or negative impression to spill over into unrelated judgments about a person, product, or institution.

Negativity bias

The tendency to give bad news, threats, criticism, and losses more psychological weight than equally sized positives.

Motivated reasoning

The tendency to use reasoning as a defense lawyer for desired conclusions rather than as an impartial search for what is most likely true.
