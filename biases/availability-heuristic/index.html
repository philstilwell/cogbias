Cognitive Bias

Availability heuristic

The tendency to judge frequency, risk, or importance by how easily examples come to mind.

Attention & Salience Quick reset Media & politicsPersonal decisions

Default mental move

Let the easiest recalled example stand in for the whole distribution.

What it distorts

It turns memory accessibility into a mistaken proxy for prevalence or probability.

Common trigger

Recent news cycles, memorable anecdotes, dramatic failures, and repetition.

First countermove

Ask for a base rate or outside-view frequency before trusting the vivid example.

Mechanism

Vivid, recent, repeated, or emotionally loaded cases are easier to retrieve, so they feel more common and more representative than they are.

Signals You Are In It

  • One memorable case is doing most of the persuasive work.
  • The perceived risk changes sharply after recent exposure.
  • Examples are plentiful but representative data is thin.

Counter It

From detection to intervention

Bias work is less about slapping on a label and more about changing what happens next in the decision process.

Spot It

  • Ask whether the current estimate is based on retrieval ease rather than a counted sample.
  • Check whether a recent dramatic event is distorting the sense of frequency.
  • Look for missing quiet cases that never became memorable.

Slow It

  • List the reference class before discussing the anecdote.
  • Search for denominator information, not just striking cases.
  • Use longer time windows than the last week or last headline.

Reframe It

  • Treat ease of recall as a clue about salience, not about rate.
  • Replace 'I can picture it' with 'How often does it actually happen?'
  • Ask what a boring spreadsheet would likely show.
