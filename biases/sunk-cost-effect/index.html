Cognitive Bias

Sunk cost effect

The tendency to keep investing in a losing path because of what has already been spent, even when the forward-looking case has weakened.

Choice & Commitment Team protocol Personal decisionsTeams & management

Default mental move

Treat past spending as a reason to continue rather than as an unrecoverable cost.

What it distorts

It keeps projects, habits, and strategies alive for backward-looking reasons rather than forward-looking ones.

Common trigger

Public commitments, long-running projects, and high personal ownership.

First countermove

Ask what you would do if the past investment had been made by someone else and could not be recovered.

Mechanism

People hate waste, identity loss, and public admission of error. Past investment becomes psychologically fused with current justification.

Signals You Are In It

  • The defense of the project begins with how much has already been spent.
  • Exit feels like admitting humiliation rather than changing course.
  • Stopping rules were never defined before the investment started.

Why This Category Matters

Biases here make us cling to defaults, past investments, and emotionally protected options.

Which option feels safer merely because it is familiar, costly to exit, or already underway?

Counter It

From detection to intervention

Bias work is less about slapping on a label and more about changing what happens next in the decision process.

Spot It

  • Ask whether any forward-looking reason remains once prior costs are ignored.
  • Check whether continuation is being justified by pride, face, or sunk effort.
  • Look for the missing stop rule.

Slow It

  • Review options from today forward only.
  • Create explicit continuation criteria before the next round of investment.
  • Let a less invested reviewer make the retain-or-exit recommendation.

Reframe It

  • Treat exit as conservation of future resources, not disrespect for past effort.
  • Ask what course would maximize value from this point onward.
  • Normalize stopping as evidence of governance rather than weakness.
