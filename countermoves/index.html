Countermoves

Process repairs worth standardizing

A bias site becomes useful when it does more than point at a flaw. These are starter moves for turning diagnosis into better decisions, cleaner postmortems, and sturdier sampling habits.

Generate Rival Hypotheses

Before gathering more evidence, force yourself to name at least two live alternatives to the story you already like.

  • Write the favored explanation in one sentence.
  • Write two rival explanations that would fit the same facts.
  • List one observation that would favor each rival.
Confirmation biasMotivated reasoningHindsight bias

Estimate Before Exposure

Put down an independent estimate before seeing the first quoted number, forecast, or recommendation.

  • Record your own guess first.
  • Only then reveal the offered anchor or outside estimate.
  • Compare the difference and explain why you moved.
Anchoring effectOverconfidence effectStatus quo bias

Use Base Rates And Reference Classes

Anchor judgments to how often similar events happen in the wider world before focusing on the vivid local story.

  • Identify the broad reference class first.
  • Write down the outside-view frequency or base rate.
  • Only then adjust for special local evidence.
Availability heuristicBase-rate neglectSurvivorship bias

Separate Person From Situation

Treat traits, incentives, constraints, and local pressures as separate variables rather than one fused impression.

  • Name the behavior you observed.
  • List three situational pressures that could also explain it.
  • Delay any trait verdict until the pressures are examined.
Fundamental attribution errorHalo effectNegativity bias

Treat Sunk Costs As Sunk

Ask what you would do if the time, money, and pride already spent could not be recovered because in reality they cannot.

  • Ignore prior investment for one pass of the decision.
  • Re-evaluate the options from today forward only.
  • Create an explicit stopping rule before continuing.
Sunk cost effectStatus quo biasOverconfidence effect

Use Decision Journals And Premortems

Capture what you believed before the outcome, then imagine in advance how the plan could fail.

  • Write the current forecast, assumptions, and confidence level.
  • Run a premortem: imagine the plan failed and list why.
  • Review the notes later before narrating the outcome.
Hindsight biasOutcome biasOverconfidence effect

Why this section exists

Fallacy sites can lean heavily on naming. Bias sites need to lean harder on process because the same person can sincerely know the label and still reproduce the distortion in planning, hiring, reviewing, and forecasting.

What to add later

Decision journals, printable checklists, calibration prompts, and team-review templates would naturally extend this section once the main taxonomy and voice settle.
